Research Interests

I don't just want to discover biological truths; I want to build with them. To me, bioengineering is where biology stops being just about observation and starts being about construction. I am fascinated by how engineered living systems can "hack" natural processes, like shRNA mimics bypassing nuclear detection, and how we can treat biological molecules as machine components.

Synthetic Biology

Engineering Stability in Living Systems

Within synthetic biology, I am focused on the problem of mutational drift in engineered genetic circuits.

Core Question

How can we design and engineer genetic circuits that are stable under heavy metabolic stress and remain functional for several generations without positive selection?

This interests me because it's a perfect example of the "Hydra" problem in biology: bacteria evolve to break our designs. In my own work on diabetic wound healing, I ran into this wall when my engineered function would degrade within days. I want to figure out how we can design circuits that constitute robust "living medicines" that can survive this evolutionary pressure.

Signal Fidelity

How can we engineer mechanisms within the cell to effectively distinguish between false positives and legitimate environmental signals?

Precision is critical in pathology. My "Smart Bacteria" project utilized a pH threshold, but bodily mechanics are complex. I want to develop more sophisticated logic gates (AND/OR/NOT) that can filter noise from true pathological signals.

Structural Biology

Intrinsically Disordered Proteins (IDPs)

My current fascination is the "dark matter" of biology: the structures we can't capture with crystallography. I'm intrigued by the 90% of the proteome that is hidden, specifically Intrinsically Disordered Proteins (IDPs) that lack a fixed 3D structure but still perform complex signaling functions.

Fuzzy Complexes

How do IDPs utilize specific "fuzzy complexes" to act as signaling regions that bind to multiple molecules with high specificity, despite low affinity?

Therapeutic Strategy

Is it possible to identify cryptic transient pockets within proteins to design small molecules that stabilize their functional state rather than inhibit it?

This challenges the standard inhibition model of drug development. If we could nudge a disordered protein back into a healthy state, it could completely change how we treat neurodegenerative diseases.

Past Project

Personalized Diabetic Wound Healing

ISEF Finalist 2025

Designed a "Smart Bacterium" (E. coli) engineered with a dual-sensing genetic circuit (pH and Glucose) to secrete DNase I and IL-10. Utilized Benchling for plasmid design, PhysiCell (C++) for agent-based modeling of biofilm reduction, and NetLogo for safety switch simulations.
