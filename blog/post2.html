Coding Nature

Why I fell in love with computational biology. It's the "spaghetti code" of life—messy, redundant, and beautiful.

We often talk about biology as a "hard science," defined by rigid taxonomies. But when I started trying to simulate it using code—first with NetLogo and then with PhysiCell—I realized that biology is actually closer to "spaghetti code." It is messy, redundant, and beautifully chaotic.

The "Bug" in the System
During my research stability testing, I spent weeks debugging a specific behavior: my simulated bacteria were supposed to secrete an enzyme when pH dropped. In my code, it was a simple `if` statement. But in the simulation, they would sometimes trigger too early due to local diffusion gradients I hadn't accounted for.

I realized I wasn't just coding a program; I was coding an agent. An autonomous entity that had to "sense" its world. The bug wasn't in the syntax; it was in my oversimplification of nature.

Modeling forces us to define our assumptions. You can't hand-wave a mechanism in C++. You have to define the rate, the threshold, the decay. It demands a level of precision that makes the eventual transition to the wet lab much more rigorous. To me, this is the art form of the 21st century.
