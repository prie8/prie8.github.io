Navigating Identity in STEM

Overcoming the "imposter syndrome" and the noise of external expectations. Why keeping your own scorecard is the most important metric.

Starting high-level research as a high schooler feels a bit like walking into a room where everyone speaks a language you're just learning. You feel the pressure to prove you belong, to show that you can handle the "real science."

I remember receiving a rejection early on where I was told to focus on "girl things." It was a jarring reminder that stereotypes still exist. It stung (right behind the sternum, as I like to say), but it also clarified my purpose.

I realized that my identity as a young woman in STEM isn't a hurdle; it's a perspective. I bring a different set of questions to the table. I bring the resilience of my grandmother and aunt, who faced their own systemic barriers.

Navigating this identity means building a "Community of Peers," finding others who share that drive to "nerd out" on science regardless of background. It means understanding that failure is data, not a definition of worth. I'm building my confidence one experiment, one line of code, and one blog post at a time.
