The Discipline of Research

Connecting the rigor of competitive swimming to the scientific process. How staring at the black line in the pool taught me to push through failure.

I often compare research to my experience as a competitive swimmer. In swimming, you spend hours staring at a black line on the bottom of the pool. You practice the same stroke, the same turn, thousands of times. Most days, you don't get a "personal best." You just get tired.

Research is similar. 90% of it is the "black line"—running the simulation again, debugging the code, pipetting the solution. It's repetitive and often filled with micro-failures.

My flute practice taught me the same lesson. "Expel air too fast, repel a note far too high." It demands a precision that only comes from discipline. You can't rush mastery.

This discipline was crucial when I faced rejection from a professor early in my research journey. The feedback was harsh, but because I was used to the "grind" of practice, I didn't see it as a stop sign. I saw it as a coaching critique. I went back to the "pool"—my code—and refined my technique until it worked.

Discipline isn't about motivation; it's about consistency in the face of boredom and failure.
